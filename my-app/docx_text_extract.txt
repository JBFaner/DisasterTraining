In the Philippines, Local Government Units (LGUs) play a critical role in ensuring community safety and preparedness. Barangay San Agustin, Novaliches, Quezon City, is no exception. Despite its ongoing efforts, the barangay still faces difficulties in delivering effective disaster response and mitigation due to challenges such as limited resources, outdated procedures, and insufficient training for disaster management personnel.Traditional disaster preparedness training within the barangay often relies on manual simulations. While these activities provide essential learning, they also come with several limitations. Scenarios are typically fixed and lack flexibility, making it difficult to adjust to real-time changes such as evolving weather conditions, varying disaster intensities, or shifts in human behavior. The effectiveness of these simulations also heavily depends on the facilitators’ experience and creativity, which can result in repetitive or predictable scenarios that do not fully assess the readiness of the response teams.As disasters grow more frequent and more severe, the need for Barangay San Agustin to adopt more effective and adaptable preparedness methods becomes increasingly urgent. The barangay must be equipped to respond to a wide range of emergencies from typhoons and flooding to earthquakes, fire incidents, and other hazards using training exercises that reflect dynamic, realistic, and complex situations.However, designing such diverse and detailed scenarios manually requires significant time and resources. This underscores the need for a solution that can automatically generate realistic disaster scenarios without adding pressure to the barangay’s limited manpower. This is where Artificial Intelligence (AI) can provide meaningful support.The Gemini API, an advanced AI technology, offers a promising approach to enhancing disaster preparedness training. It can generate highly detailed, realistic, and adaptable disaster scenarios in real time. Through machine learning, it can adjust simulations based on environmental data, geographic conditions, and probable human responses. This results in training environments that are far more dynamic and unpredictable compared to traditional methods.Integrating the Gemini API into Barangay San Agustin’s disaster preparedness program would allow the barangay to conduct more frequent, diverse, and immersive training sessions. The system’s ability to process a wide range of variables helps create scenarios that evolve with changing conditions effectively challenging response teams to make quick, informed decisions under realistic pressure. Additionally, AI-powered simulations are more cost-effective and scalable, reducing the time and resources needed to produce high-quality training exercises.By adopting AI-generated scenario simulations, Barangay San Agustin, Novaliches, Quezon City, can significantly strengthen its disaster preparedness capacity. This approach can enhance team readiness, improve resource management, and ultimately contribute to protecting the lives and safety of its residents during emergencies.1.2 Context and Scope  The project is initiated within the critical operational framework of Local Government Unit 4 (LGU 4), a jurisdiction facing frequent threats from Flooding and Typhoons, Fires. Effective and timely disaster response is paramount, yet traditional preparedness drills often suffer from:Repetitiveness: Scenarios are static and predictable, leading to rote memorization rather than adaptive decision-making.Limited Scope: Drills rarely cover the full spectrum of complex, cascading failures common in real-world events (e.g., simultaneous communications failure and infrastructure damage).To address these shortcomings, this project proposes the development of a web-based training platform. This platform is strategically designed to shift the LGU's focus from mere procedural compliance to adaptive decision-making under pressure. By integrating the Gemini API for automated scenario generation, the environment will dynamically introduce localized, novel, and diverse challenges, thus significantly enhancing the LGU's response capabilities and overall resilience against unpredictable disasters.Scope (Inclusions)The following deliverables and activities are included within the project's scope:Design and Development of a Functional Simulation Platform: Creation of a secure, web-based system capable of hosting and running disaster response scenarios.Integration of the Gemini API for Scenario Auto-Generation: Implementation of the Google Gemini API to automatically generate diverse, geographically localized, and technically complex disaster scenarios tailored for LGU 4.Development of a Scoring Mechanism: Creation of a quantitative system to evaluate LGU personnel response decisions, timelines, and resource allocation effectiveness during the simulation.Validation through a Pilot Training Session: Execution of a formal pilot simulation session with select LGU 4 personnel to test the platform's functionality and validate the effectiveness of the training methodology.Exclusions (Out of Scope)Full Integration with LGU's Actual Operational Disaster Monitoring Systems: The platform will use static or pre-determined data points for the simulation; it will not integrate with real-time external data feeds (e.g., live weather feeds, actual infrastructure monitoring).Deployment to Other Local Government Units (LGUs): The system's design and deployment are strictly limited to LGU 4.Development of a Virtual Reality (VR) or Augmented Reality (AR) Environment: The training platform will be entirely web-based (standard 2D interface) and will not include any immersive VR or AR components.1.3 Problem StatementLocal Government Unit 4 (LGU 4) currently faces a critical gap in its disaster preparedness strategy: existing simulation and training protocols are static, resource-intensive to update, and fail to adequately prepare personnel for the complex, diverse, and unpredictable nature of multi-hazard disaster scenarios.Specifically, the problem is threefold:1. Lack of Scenario Diversity and Realism: Current drills are often based on predictable, repetitive scripts, which leads to procedural complacency among personnel rather than fostering adaptive, critical decision-making required when faced with cascading failures (e.g., simultaneous infrastructure damage, communication outages, and mass displacement). 2. Inefficient Scenario Generation: The manual creation, validation, and maintenance of diverse, localized training scenarios consume excessive time and human resources, limiting the frequency and quality of preparedness exercises. 3. Subjective Performance Evaluation: The absence of a standardized, quantitative scoring mechanism results in subjective after-action reviews, making it difficult to objectively measure improvements in LGU response competency over time.This project, utilizing the Gemini API for automated scenario auto-generation, directly addresses this deficiency by creating a dynamic, scalable, and objective simulation environment to ensure LGU 4 personnel are trained to handle the full spectrum of high-stakes, localized, and complex disaster events.1.4 Objectives and Goals Objectives (Specific Outcomes for LGU Personnel Readiness)The immediate mission is to equip LGU 4 personnel with the best possible tools, ensuring they are prepared for the unpredictable challenges of a real disaster. Instead of trying to plan the entire project for a year and hoping nothing changes, Scrum breaks the work into short, intense bursts called Sprints (usually 2-4 weeks). At the end of every Sprint, we deliver a small, functional piece of the simulation platform.The core idea is: Work with speed, show what you’ve built, and adapt immediately based on feedback. It’s about being flexible and focusing on continuous improvement.Why Scrum Works for This ProjectWe need Scrum because our project has two big unknowns:The New Technology Challenge: Integrating the Gemini API is cutting-edge. We don't know exactly how LGU data will best connect with the AI right from day one. Scrum lets us test the connection in a short Sprint, see what works, fix what doesn't, and keep moving forward without getting stuck. It allows for smart experimentation.Making Scenarios Feel Real: The most crucial test is whether the LGU personnel feel the scenarios are realistic. With Scrum, we don't wait until the end to show them the platform. Every few weeks, we can demo new scenarios and ask: "Does this feel like a real disaster? What needs to change?" This constant, hands-on feedback loop with the LGU team guarantees we build a training tool they actually need and trust.How We Work Together (The Scrum Roles)The Voice of the LGU (Product Owner): This person represents the training needs of LGU 4. They tell the team what is most important to build next for effective disaster preparedness.The Team Coach (Scrum Master): This person helps the development team stay focused and removes any roadblocks whether technical issues or confusing requirements so the team can work smoothly.The Builders (Development Team): This is the Capstone team that designs, codes, and tests the platform. They decide how to turn the LGU's needs into a working piece of software every Sprint.In short, Scrum is the project's foundation that guarantees collaboration, adaptation, and rapid learning, ensuring the final platform is effective, relevant, and fully accepted by the LGU users.2.2 Enterprise Architecture ConceptsBefore we write a single line of code, we need a reliable plan. Enterprise Architecture (EA) is simply our way of making sure our cutting-edge AI training system isn't just a cool tech demo, it's a sustainable, smart solution that genuinely fits how the LGU works.Think of EA as the Master Plan that guarantees our LGU's mission (fast, effective disaster response) is perfectly aligned with the technology we build (our Gemini API system).A. Our Core Mission: Bridging the GapOur main goal with EA is to draw a clear line from where the LGU is right now (their current training methods) to where they need to be in the future (using our smart, dynamic AI simulations).It's about the Big Picture: It stops us from building something amazing that, six months later, doesn't connect to their existing data or IT infrastructure.B. The Four Cornerstones of Our DesignTo make sure we don't miss anything, we analyze the LGU's organization through four simple lenses (our "domains"):Business Architecture (The "People & Process" Plan):Focus: We start with the LGU's current manual: How do they respond to a flood? We model their existing disaster protocols, organizational hierarchy, and training needs.Goal: To ensure our simulation tool directly supports and improves the exact steps LGU personnel need to take when a real disaster hits.Data Architecture (The "Information Flow" Plan):Focus: We look at all the crucial data: historical incident reports, LGU personnel lists, and the hazard maps.Goal: To design a data structure that allows the Gemini AI to pull in relevant real-world data to create realistic scenarios and, critically, ensures that the training feedback data is stored securely and accessibly.Application Architecture (The "Software Blueprint" Plan):Focus: This is the map of our software. It defines the Training Platform itself, the Reporting Module, and how these pieces integrate.Goal: To show precisely how the Gemini API is plugged into the main simulation platform—minimizing complexity and ensuring a smooth, reliable exchange of information.Technology Architecture (The "Infrastructure" Plan):Focus: The fundamental technology underneath it all: the cloud server, the network connection, and the specific version of the programming language.Goal: To set the technical standards (e.g., security, performance) that guarantee the platform can handle many users running complex simulations without crashing.C. Why Frameworks MatterUsing established guidelines (like TOGAF) means we don't have to invent our design process. It forces us through a structured cycle (the ADM), ensuring we systematically check all four domains before we move to development.The Capstone Value: This formal approach makes our Capstone project far more professional. It proves that our technical solution is not just an isolated piece of software, but a strategic asset designed to integrate seamlessly into a real-world organization.2.3 Microservices Architecture Imagine the old way of building software (a Monolith) was like building a single, giant, multi-story skyscraper that contained the entire LGU training system: the offices, the server room, the cafeteria, and the gym. If the cafeteria plumbing burst, the whole building had to shut down for repairs.Microservices is the modern approach. We don't build a skyscraper; we build a campus of small, specialized buildings, and they all talk to each other via secure, fast pathways (APIs).A. Why We Choose Separate BuildingsWe break down the LGU training system into small, focused services, each handling a specific job:Specialized Service (The "Building")Job DescriptionExample of Its IndependenceUser Authentication ServiceChecks user IDs and passwords.Uses a database optimized for security checks.Simulation Core ServiceRuns the actual disaster scenario logistics.Uses a powerful server optimized for speed.AI Scenario Generator ServiceTalks directly to the Gemini API to create dynamic events.We can quickly update this service if Google changes the Gemini API, without touching the rest of the system.Reporting ServiceGenerates training reports and performance metrics.We can use a unique reporting technology here without making the other services use it.2.4 DevOps and CI/CD The development of the LGU Disaster Preparedness system utilizes DevOps practices to bridge the gap between software development and IT operations. This ensures that the platform is not only built efficiently but also remains stable, secure, and easily updatable as new disaster simulation needs arise.Continuous Integration and Continuous Delivery (CI/CD)The project employs a CI/CD pipeline to automate the path from code commit to deployment.Continuous Integration (CI): Every time a developer commits code to the repository, automated builds and unit tests are triggered to ensure that new changes do not break existing functionality.Continuous Delivery (CD): Once the code passes the testing phase and is approved, it is automatically merged and deployed to the staging or production environments, allowing for a rapid and reliable release cycle.Infrastructure as Code (IaC)To maintain consistency across development, staging, and production environments, the team uses Infrastructure as Code (IaC).This approach defines the cloud infrastructure (such as servers and network configurations) through machine-readable files rather than manual configuration.By treating infrastructure like application code, the team can provision and manage environments automatically through the CI/CD pipeline, reducing human error and ensuring environment parity.Monitoring and AlertingMaintaining high availability for a disaster training platform is critical. The project implements a comprehensive Monitoring and Alerting strategy using a Kubernetes-based stack:Metric Collection: Tools like Prometheus collect real-time data from the system, including server health and application performance.Visualization: Grafana is utilized to create dashboards that visualize system metrics for the development and operations team.Real-time Alerting: An Alertmanager is configured to send immediate notifications to communication channels, such as Microsoft Teams, if the system encounters errors or performance degradation, ensuring a proactive response to technical issues.2.5 Relevant Studies and ResearchThe transition toward intelligent disaster risk reduction and management (DRRM) requires a shift from static, paper-based protocols to dynamic, automated systems. Current research highlights that localized readiness is often the "weak link" in national resilience, and modern software architectures combined with Large Language Models (LLMs) are now the primary solution for bridging this gap.Local StudiesBaltazar, Florencio, Vicente, and Belizario (2024), in their study "The Role of Artificial Intelligence in Disaster Prediction, Mitigation, and Response in the Philippines: Challenges and Opportunities," confirm that while the Philippine government has integrated AI into national frameworks, local-level adoption is hindered by infrastructure and budget constraints. Their findings advocate for AI-driven initiatives that can assist in post-disaster assessments and enhance the accuracy of localized early warning protocols.Philippine Institute for Development Studies [PIDS] (2025), in the updated report "Disaster Preparedness and Local Governance in the Philippines," notes that even with strong laws like RA 10121, there remains a disconnect in how national policies "cascade" to the subnational and LGU levels. The study concludes that for resilience to be effective, local governance must be supported by functional tools that translate theoretical policy into actionable, simulated exercises.Foreign StudiesWang, Wang, Zhang, and Zeng (2025), in the paper "Towards Intelligent Emergency Management: A Scenario Learning Decision Framework Enabled by Large Language Models," demonstrate that LLMs can construct evolving emergency contexts, including evacuation routes and situational graphs. Their research validates that AI-generated scenarios move beyond the governance challenges of "fragmented strategies and cognitive disconnection" found in traditional, human-led drills.Narváez, Battaglia, Fernández, and Rossi (2025), in their systematic literature review "Designing Microservices Using AI," analyze how decoupled architectures optimize performance and mitigate the inherent complexities of scalable systems. Their study provides the technical justification for using microservices in public safety applications, as it ensures fault isolation and independent deployability critical for keeping government training portals available during emergencies.2.6 Integration of Information Systems in Enterprise EnvironmentThe successful deployment of the LGU Disaster Preparedness system requires integrating the new platform with existing LGU data sources and external technologies (like the Gemini API). This section describes the methods used and the common challenges encountered when linking disparate systems to form a cohesive enterprise solution.A. Core Objectives of IntegrationIntegration aims to ensure seamless data flow, process consistency, and application interoperability across the enterprise. For the LGU project, the main objectives are:1.Unified Data Access: Allowing the simulation platform to access crucial LGU datasets (e.g., population density, asset locations) for accurate scenario generation.2.Process Automation: Automating the handover of training results from the simulation platform to the LGU's HR/Training records system.3.Real-Time Interoperability: Enabling the dynamic, two-way communication required to send prompts to the Gemini API and receive scenario text in real time.B. Key Integration MethodsThe microservices architecture chosen for this project naturally aligns with modern, loosely coupled integration patterns:1.Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) / API Gateway:Method: Utilizing APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) as the standardized communication mechanism between systems.Application: Our microservices communicate with each other and with the Gemini API using RESTful APIs. An API Gateway acts as a single entry point for all external access, managing security and routing traffic to the correct service.2.Database Integration (For Legacy Systems):Method: Directly accessing or replicating data from a legacy system's database.Challenge: This is generally avoided in microservices, but may be necessary if the LGU has old, critical systems that cannot expose data via an API. It creates tight coupling and complexity.3.Message Passing / Event-Driven Architecture (EDA):Method: Systems communicate asynchronously by sending "events" (messages) through a central Message Broker (e.g., Kafka, RabbitMQ).Application: This is ideal for handling non-immediate data flow, such as sending a log of a completed training session to the LGU's central logging system without needing an immediate response.C. Integration Challenges in the LGU EnvironmentSuccessfully integrating the system requires overcoming several common enterprise challenges:1.Technical Diversity (Heterogeneity):Challenge: Integrating systems built on different technologies (e.g., a modern cloud-based platform vs. an older LGU system written in a legacy language).Mitigation: Standardizing on JSON/XML data formats and RESTful API contracts acts as a universal translator between technologies.2.Data Semantic Inconsistency:Challenge: Different systems may use the same term to mean different things (e.g., one system defines "Citizen ID" differently than another).Mitigation: Developing a unified Canonical Data Model that acts as the single source of truth for key LGU entities (Personnel, Assets, Hazards).3.Security and Governance:Challenge: Ensuring secure data transmission and authentication across systems, especially when involving sensitive LGU citizen data or proprietary external APIs (like Gemini).Mitigation: Implementing the API Gateway to enforce security policies (like OAuth 2.0 authentication) and encrypting all data transmission via TLS/SSL.Chapter 3METHODOLOGY3.1 Agile Scrum Methodology in the ProjectThe Agile Scrum Framework was selected to manage the development of the LGU Disaster Preparedness system. Scrum is an empirical, iterative, and incremental approach designed to manage complex projects, providing the flexibility needed to handle evolving requirements, integrate new technologies (like the Gemini API), and adapt to stakeholder feedback from the Local Government Unit (LGU).A. Core Principles AppliedThe project adhered to the core pillars of Scrum:Transparency: All work, progress, and issues (the Product Backlog and Sprint Backlog) were visible to the Development Team, the Product Owner (LGU representative), and the Scrum Master.Inspection: Progress was regularly inspected through the Daily Scrum and the Sprint Review meeting.Adaptation: The team continuously adapted its process and plan in the Sprint Retrospective based on what was learned during the previous iteration.B. Project Roles and ResponsibilitiesThe three defined Scrum roles were essential for managing the project's scope and quality (as detailed in Section 3.2):Product Owner: Acted as the voice of the LGU client, prioritizing features that maximized value (e.g., prioritizing flood scenarios over drought scenarios based on LGU risk analysis).Scrum Master: Facilitated the Scrum process, coached the team, and removed impediments, particularly technical blockers related to API key management or cloud dependencies.Development Team: A self-organizing, cross-functional group responsible for all tasks, including designing the architecture, writing code, and performing integration testing of the Gemini API.C. The Scrum Events (Meetings)The project utilized the five standard Scrum events to structure work into focused iterations called Sprints (typically two weeks long):Figure no. 1 Agile Scrum Framework1.Sprint Planning: The team met to select the highest priority items from the Product Backlog and define the Sprint Goal (e.g., "Implement the successful generation and display of a basic fire-related scenario").2.Daily Scrum (Stand-up): A brief, 15-minute meeting held daily to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal, identify any immediate roadblocks (impediments), and synchronize work.3.Development Work: The team built the features, integrated the Gemini API, and conducted continuous testing.4.Sprint Review: A formal meeting where the team demonstrated the "Done" Increment (e.g., a working scenario auto-generation feature) to the Product Owner and LGU stakeholders to gather feedback.5.Sprint Retrospective: An internal team meeting to inspect how the last Sprint went regarding people, relationships, tools, and processes, leading to concrete improvements for the next Sprint.D. Artifacts (Work Products)Three key artifacts were maintained to track and manage the project:Product Backlog: A prioritized, dynamic list of all known features, functions, requirements, and enhancements needed for the LGU Training System.Sprint Backlog: The subset of the Product Backlog selected for the current Sprint, plus the plan for delivering the Increment.Increment: The usable, integrated result of all Sprint Backlog items completed during the Sprint and all previous Sprints, which was always in a potentially shippable state.The adoption of Scrum ensured that the capstone project delivered a high-value, working solution that constantly incorporated the LGU's needs and technical findings from the Gemini API integration.3.2 Roles and Responsibilities Each role in the project team carries significant responsibilities that directly influence the success and overall performance of the capstone project. Business ArchitectureFocus: This domain models existing disaster protocols, organizational hierarchy, and training needs based on the LGU's current manual (e.g., their specific response to a flood).Goal: To ensure the simulation tool directly supports and improves the exact steps personnel must take during a real disaster.Stakeholder Representation: The Product Owner (represented by Barangay San Agustin) defines the specific disaster scenarios and ensures the tool addresses real-world emergency gaps.2. Data ArchitectureFocus: This lens examines crucial information such as historical incident reports, LGU personnel lists, and hazard maps.Goal: To design a data structure that allows the Gemini AI to pull in relevant real-world data for realistic scenarios and ensures training feedback is stored securely and accessibly.Integration: A unified Canonical Data Model is used to mitigate semantic inconsistencies across different systems.3. Application ArchitectureFocus: This serves as the software blueprint, defining the training platform, reporting module, and how these components integrate.Goal: To define precisely how the Gemini API is plugged into the main platform to minimize complexity and ensure reliable information exchange.Design Pattern: The project uses a Microservices Architecture, breaking the system into specialized services (e.g., Authentication, Simulation Core, AI Scenario Generator) that communicate via APIs.4. Technology ArchitectureFocus: This covers the fundamental underlying technology, including cloud servers (such as Firebase), network connections, and programming languages.Goal: To set technical standards for security and performance, ensuring the platform can handle multiple users without crashing.Security Measures: Includes implementing an API Gateway to enforce security policies like OAuth 2.0 and encrypting data transmission via TLS/SSL.CHAPTERIV4Requirements Analysis4.1.
