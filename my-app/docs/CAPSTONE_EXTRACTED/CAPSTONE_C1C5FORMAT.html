Chapter 1

INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background of the Capstone Project

Disasters, whether natural or human-made, continue to pose serious challenges across the world. In the Philippines, Local Government Units (LGUs) play a critical role in ensuring community safety and preparedness. Barangay San Agustin, Novaliches, Quezon City, is no exception. Despite its ongoing efforts, the barangay still faces difficulties in delivering effective disaster response and mitigation due to challenges such as limited resources, outdated procedures, and insufficient training for disaster management personnel.

Traditional disaster preparedness training in the  barangay often relies on manual simulations and drills. While these activities provide essential learning opportunities, they also carry several limitations. Scenarios are usually fixed and lack flexibility, making it difficult to adjust to real-time changes such as evolving weather conditions, varying disaster intensities, or unpredictable human behavior. The effectiveness of these simulations also heavily depends on the facilitators’ experience and creativity, which can result in repetitive, predictable scenarios that fail to fully assess and enhance the readiness of the response teams.

As disasters grow more frequent and more severe, the need for Barangay San Agustin to adopt more effective and adaptable preparedness methods has become urgent. The barangay must be equipped to respond to a wide range of emergencies, from typhoons and flooding to earthquakes, fire, and other hazards, using training exercises that reflect dynamic, realistic, and complex situations.

Designing such diverse and detailed scenarios manually, however, demands considerable time and expertise. This highlights the need for a solution that can automatically generate realistic disaster scenarios without increasing the burden on the barangay’s limited manpower. In this context, Artificial Intelligence (AI) offers meaningful and practical avenues for support.

The Gemini API, an advanced AI technology, provides a promising approach to enhancing disaster preparedness training. It can generate highly detailed, realistic, and adaptable disaster scenarios in  near real time. Through machine learning capabilities, it can adjust simulations based on environmental data, geographic conditions, and probable human responses. This results in training environments that are far more dynamic and unpredictable compared to those produced by traditional methods.

Integrating the Gemini API into Barangay San Agustin’s disaster preparedness program enables the barangay to conduct more frequent, varied, and immersive training sessions. The system’s capacity to process a wide range of variables helps create scenarios that evolve with changing conditions, effectively challenging response teams to make quick, informed decisions under realistic pressure. Additionally, AI-powered simulations are more cost-effective and scalable, reducing the time and resources required to produce high-quality training exercises.

By adopting AI-generated scenario simulations, Barangay San Agustin can significantly strengthen its disaster preparedness capacity. This approach can enhance team readiness, improve resource management, and ultimately contribute to safeguarding the lives and welfare of residents during emergencies.

1.2 Context and Scope 

This project is rooted in the real needs of Local Government Unit 4 (LGU 4), a community that consistently faces threats from typhoons, heavy flooding, fires, and related hazards. While disaster response is a top priority, the traditional drills used to train personnel often encounter two major limitations:

  1. Predictability: Because drills are static and repetitive, personnel may end up memorizing scripted steps rather than learning how to think critically and adapt during an evolving crisis.
  2. Narrow Focus: Conventional drills often fail to capture the “chaos factor” of real disasters, such as cascading failures in power, communications, transportation, or access to critical resources.

To address these issues, the researchers are developing a web-based training platform that shifts the focus from merely following a manual to making informed, adaptive decisions under pressure. By leveraging the Gemini API, the system will generate localized, diverse, and dynamic scenarios on demand, thereby increasing the community’s resilience to unpredictable disaster events.

Scope of the Project

To ensure a focused and manageable delivery, the project centers on four main areas:

  1. The Simulation Platform: Development of a secure, web-based system where disaster scenarios can be created, hosted, and executed. This includes interfaces for administrators, trainers, and participants.
  2. AI Integration: Implementation of the Google Gemini API to automatically generate complex, context-aware disaster scenarios tailored to the specific geography and risk profile of LGU 4.
  3. A Scoring System: Design of a quantitative evaluation framework to measure participant performance, focusing on decision-making speed, appropriateness of actions, and resource allocation during simulated events.
  4. Real-World Testing: Conduct a formal pilot session with LGU 4 personnel to validate the platform’s functionality, scenario realism, and training effectiveness, followed by refinement based on user feedback.

Project Exclusions

To keep the scope realistic and achievable within the constraints of time, budget, and academic requirements, the following items are explicitly excluded:

  • Live Data Integration: The system will rely on predefined and curated datasets for simulations. It will not be connected to real-time weather feeds, sensor networks, or live infrastructure monitoring systems.
  • Expansion to Other Units: The initial design, deployment, and evaluation are limited to LGU 4. Adaptation and scaling to other LGUs are considered potential future work.
  • VR/AR Components: The platform is designed as a 2D web-based interface. Virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) environments are beyond the scope of this project.

1.3 Problem Statement

Local Government Unit 4 (LGU 4) currently faces a critical gap in its disaster preparedness strategy. Existing simulation and training protocols are static, time-consuming to update, and do not adequately prepare personnel for the complex, diverse, and unpredictable nature of multi-hazard disaster scenarios. More specifically, the problem can be described in three parts:

  1. Lack of Scenario Diversity and Realism: Current drills are often based on predictable, repetitive scripts. This fosters procedural complacency rather than the adaptive, critical thinking needed in real crises involving cascading failures such as simultaneous infrastructure damage, communication outages, and population displacement.
  2. Inefficient Scenario Generation: Creating, validating, and maintaining diverse, localized training scenarios manually requires substantial time and human effort. This reduces the frequency, quality, and variety of preparedness exercises that LGU 4 can realistically conduct.
  3. Subjective Performance Evaluation: The absence of a standardized, quantitative scoring framework leads to subjective after-action reviews. This makes it difficult to objectively track improvements in response competence and to identify specific areas for development over time.

This project, through the use of the Gemini API for automated scenario generation, seeks to address these deficiencies by creating a dynamic, scalable, and objective simulation environment. The ultimate aim is to ensure that LGU 4 personnel are trained to handle a wide spectrum of high-stakes, localized, and complex disaster events.

1.4 Objectives and Goals

Objectives (Immediate Outcomes)

Grounded in recent research (e.g., Wang et al., 2025) on the role of Large Language Models (LLMs) in emergency management, this project has the following immediate objectives:

  • A Reliable Training Sandbox: To design and deploy a flexible, web-based platform that serves as a safe environment where LGU personnel can repeatedly practice critical decision-making without any risk to actual communities.
  • AI-Driven Unpredictability: To integrate the Gemini API so that each training session generates unique, context-sensitive scenarios. This directly addresses procedural complacency by requiring participants to think critically and adapt to new conditions in every exercise.
  • Stress-Test with Complexity Scenarios: To conduct and document at least ten distinct complex simulation runs (e.g., coastal storms combined with communication blackouts) to demonstrate the platform’s ability to model cascading failures and interdependent risks.
  • Apply User-Centered Design: To refine the system through iterative pilot sessions with Barangay San Agustin staff, ensuring that the final platform is practical, usable, and aligned with the actual workflows of disaster response personnel.

Broader Goals (Community Impact)

In line with the findings of the Philippine Institute for Development Studies (PIDS, 2025), which emphasize the importance of operationalizing national resilience policies at the local level, this project also aims to achieve the following long-term goals:

  • Contribute to Saving Lives Through Better Decisions: By improving the speed, coordination, and clarity of LGU responses during crises, the project aims to help minimize casualties and reduce damage to property and critical infrastructure.
  • Establish a Replicable Local Model: In response to the challenges identified by Baltazar et al. (2024) regarding local AI adoption, the project intends to position LGU 4 as a pioneer in AI-assisted disaster preparedness. The resulting architecture and methodology are designed to be scalable and adaptable as a blueprint for other LGUs.

1.5 Significance and Relevance

Local Government Unit 4

This project is fundamentally about equipping LGU 4 with modern tools to better protect its residents.

  • Building Confidence in Crisis: Instead of repeating the same drills, personnel can be trained against thousands of unique, realistic scenarios. Over time, this repetition under varying conditions builds the “muscle memory” and situational awareness needed to respond effectively in real emergencies.
  • Enabling Smarter, Faster Response: Because the platform is relatively low-cost to operate and easy to repeat, LGU 4 can conduct frequent training sessions throughout the year. This continuous practice leads to sharper skills and faster, more coordinated actions when actual disasters strike.

  • Offering a Practical Blueprint for Resilience: The project promotes a future where training is both unpredictable and adaptive, and where local governments can rely on technology to translate national policy frameworks into operational tools.

REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

2.1 Agile Scrum Methodology Overview

Scrum is an Agile framework designed to help teams build complex products incrementally while maintaining close collaboration with stakeholders. Rather than planning the entire project in detail at the beginning and hoping that conditions remain unchanged, Scrum divides the work into short, time-boxed iterations called sprints (typically 2–4 weeks). At the end of each sprint, the team delivers a small but functional part of the system.

The core idea behind Scrum is to work in short cycles, regularly show what has been built, and adapt quickly based on feedback. This continuous improvement and responsiveness to change make Scrum well-suited for innovative and technically challenging projects.

Why Scrum Fits This Project

This project involves two major uncertainties:

  1. New Technology Integration: Integrating the Gemini API into an LGU training context is relatively new. The team does not know at the outset exactly how LGU data will best interact with the AI model. Scrum allows the team to experiment in controlled sprints, trying an integration approach, evaluating results quickly, and refining both the technical design and the prompts.
  2. Scenario Realism and User Acceptance: The ultimate test of the platform is whether LGU personnel perceive the scenarios as realistic and useful. Using Scrum, the team does not wait until the end of development to reveal the system. Instead, they present new features regularly and gather feedback on whether the scenarios “feel” like real disaster situations and what adjustments are needed.

Scrum Roles in the Project

  • Product Owner (Voice of the LGU): Represents the needs and priorities of LGU 4. This role decides which features should be developed first to maximize the value of the platform for disaster preparedness.
  • Scrum Master (Team Facilitator): Ensures the Scrum framework is followed, helps remove obstacles that slow down development (such as unclear requirements or technical issues), and supports the team in staying focused on the Sprint Goal.
  • Development Team (The Builders): The capstone group responsible for analysis, design, coding, testing, and integration. The team self-organizes to decide how best to implement each requirement within the constraints of a sprint.

In summary, Scrum provides a structured yet flexible approach that promotes collaboration, rapid learning, and continuous adaptation qualities that are essential for successfully delivering an AI-enhanced disaster training platform.

2.2 Enterprise Architecture Concepts

Before implementing the system, it is essential to ensure that the solution fits the LGU’s actual operations and long-term goals. Enterprise Architecture (EA) provides a structured way to align technology initiatives with organizational strategies and processes.

In this project, EA is used as a “master plan” to ensure that the AI-driven training platform is not just a standalone technical product but a strategic tool that supports LGU 4’s mission of fast and effective disaster response.

Core Mission: Bridging the Current and Future State

The EA work in this project focuses on bridging the gap between:

  • The current state: primarily manual, script-based drills and fragmented information systems.
  • The target state: an integrated, AI-assisted simulation platform that supports continuous, data-informed training.

By explicitly mapping this transition, the team avoids developing features that cannot be supported by existing infrastructure or that do not align with LGU protocols.

Four Architectural Domains

Following common EA practices (such as those in TOGAF), the project examines LGU 4 through four key domains:

  1. Business Architecture (People and Process):
  • Focus: Existing disaster response protocols, organizational hierarchy, decision-making chains, and training requirements.
  • Goal: Ensure that the simulation platform reinforces and, where necessary, enhances the actual steps LGU personnel must follow during disasters.

  1. Data Architecture (Information Flow):
  • Focus: Critical data such as hazard maps, population data, asset inventories, and incident reports.
  • Goal: Design data models that enable the AI to generate realistic, localized scenarios and store training results securely and consistently.
  1. Application Architecture (Software Structure):
  • Focus: Logical components of the system, such as the training platform, AI scenario generator, evaluation module, and reporting tools, and how they interact.
  • Goal: Define clear interfaces and responsibilities so that the Gemini API can be integrated with minimal complexity and high reliability.
  1. Technology Architecture (Infrastructure and Platform):
  • Focus: Underlying technologies, including cloud services (e.g., Firebase), servers, networks, and development frameworks.
  • Goal: Set standards for performance, scalability, and security that support stable operation even under intensive use.

Value of Using Established Frameworks

By using a structured framework like TOGAF’s Architecture Development Method (ADM), the project avoids ad hoc decision-making. It systematically considers business, data, application, and technology aspects before moving into full-scale development. This improves integration quality and demonstrates that the capstone project is grounded in professional, enterprise-level practices.

2.3 Microservices Architecture 

Traditional monolithic systems bundle all functionalities—user authentication, business logic, reporting, and more—into a single large application. While simple to start with, such systems can become difficult to maintain and scale. A problem or change in one feature may affect the entire application.

In contrast, microservices architecture breaks the system into a set of small, independent services, each responsible for a specific function. These services communicate through well-defined APIs.

Why Microservices for the LGU Training Platform

For this project, microservices were chosen to:

  • Isolate complex functions such as AI scenario generation from general application logic.
  • allow independent development, testing, deployment, and scaling of services.
  • Make it easier to integrate with external services like the Gemini API and messaging or analytics tools.

Example Core Services

  • User Authentication Service

Handles login, registration, and role-based access control, using a security-focused data store.

  • Simulation Core Service

Manages the execution of disaster scenarios, including timelines, events, and interactions during a training session.

  • AI Scenario Generator Service

Communicates with the Gemini API to generate new, context-aware scenarios based on LGU-specific data and parameters.

  • Reporting and Analytics Service

Produces training summaries, performance metrics, and trend reports for administrators and decision-makers.

Because these services are loosely coupled, a change in one (for example, updating how prompts are structured for Gemini) does not require redeploying the entire system. This design choice supports long-term maintainability and scalability.

2.4 DevOps and CI/CD 

The development of the LGU Disaster Preparedness Training System adopts DevOps practices to bridge the gap between software development and IT operations. This ensures that the platform is not only built efficiently but can also be deployed, updated, and monitored reliably over time.

Continuous Integration and Continuous Delivery (CI/CD)

A CI/CD pipeline is used to automate the flow from code changes to deployment:

  • Continuous Integration (CI): Every time a developer commits code, automated builds and tests run to verify that the new changes do not break existing functionality. This reduces integration issues and detects bugs early.
  • Continuous Delivery (CD): Once changes pass all tests and code reviews, they are automatically packaged and deployed to staging or production environments. This enables frequent, reliable releases and rapid delivery of improvements to LGU users.

Infrastructure as Code (IaC)

To ensure consistency across development, staging, and production environments, the team uses IaC:

  • The cloud infrastructure servers, networks, firewalls, and other configurations are described in machine-readable definition files.
  • These definitions are version-controlled alongside the application code.
  • Environments can be recreated or updated automatically, minimizing manual configuration errors.

Monitoring and Alerting

Maintaining high availability is crucial, especially for a platform intended for disaster preparedness:

  • Metric Collection: Tools such as Prometheus (or equivalent) collect system metrics, including CPU usage, memory, response times, and error rates.
  • Visualization: Dashboards (e.g., using Grafana) provide real-time and historical visualizations of system health and usage.
  • Alerting: Alerting tools (e.g., Alertmanager) send notifications to channels such as email or Microsoft Teams when critical thresholds are exceeded, allowing the team to respond proactively to issues.

These DevOps practices help ensure that the disaster training platform is stable, secure, and continuously improving.

2.5 Relevant Studies and Research

Modernizing Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) requires moving from static, paper-based protocols to systems that are dynamic, data-driven, and automated. Research in both local and international contexts supports the combination of advanced software architectures and LLMs to improve preparedness.

Local Studies

  • Challenge in Local Adoption: Baltazar, Florencio, Vicente, and Belizario (2024) observe that while the Philippine government is encouraging AI use at the national level, many LGUs struggle to adopt these technologies because of financial and infrastructure constraints. They highlight the need for AI-driven tools that directly support post-disaster assessments and more accurate local warning systems.
  • Policy and Practice Gap: A 2025 report by the Philippine Institute for Development Studies (PIDS) notes a gap between strong national DRRM policies (such as RA 10121) and their practical implementation at the LGU level. It concludes that resilience becomes effective only when local governments have concrete tools that convert policy into actionable, localized training and planning.

Foreign Studies

  • AI as a Scenario Generator: Wang, Wang, Zhang, and Zeng (2025) demonstrate that LLMs can construct evolving emergency contexts, including situational graphs and adaptive evacuation routes. Their findings indicate that AI-generated scenarios often outperform traditional, static drills by reducing “fragmented strategies” and enabling more holistic training.
  • Microservices for Resilience: A systematic review by Narváez, Battaglia, Fernández, and Rossi (2025) emphasizes the advantages of microservices in building large, mission-critical systems. They argue that microservices improve performance, fault isolation, and scalability qualities that are particularly important for public safety platforms that must remain operational even under stress.

These studies collectively support the project’s choice to use LLM-based scenario generation and microservices architecture as foundational elements of an LGU-level disaster preparedness system.

2.6 Integration of Information Systems in Enterprise Environment

The deployment of the LGU Disaster Preparedness System requires seamless integration with existing LGU data sources and external technologies like the Gemini API. Integration ensures that the platform can use real operational data and that its outputs feed back into existing administrative processes.

Core Objectives of Integration

1.        Unified Data Access: Enable the simulation platform to securely access key LGU datasets (e.g., hazard maps, population density, asset locations) to support realistic scenario generation.

2.        Process Automation: Automate the transfer of training results, attendance, and evaluation data into the LGU’s HR or training records system to reduce manual encoding and errors.

3.        Real-Time Interoperability: Support bi-directional communication between the platform and external services (such as the Gemini API) to generate and update scenarios in real time during training sessions.

Key Integration Methods

1.        Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA) / API Gateway:

  • Systems communicate via standardized RESTful APIs using formats like JSON.
  • An API Gateway acts as a single secure entry point, managing authentication, rate limiting, and routing requests to individual microservices.

2.        Database Integration (For Legacy Systems):

  • Where older LGU systems cannot expose APIs, carefully controlled database integration or data replication may be used.
  • This approach is minimized due to tight coupling and maintenance complexity but acknowledged as sometimes necessary in real LGU environments.

3.        Message-Based / Event-Driven Architecture (EDA):

  • Asynchronous event messaging (using a message broker such as Kafka or RabbitMQ) supports non-blocking communication.
  • For example, when a training session is completed, an event is published to record the session in central logs or analytics systems without delaying user feedback.

Integration Challenges in the LGU Environment

1.        Technical Heterogeneity:

Different systems may be built on various technologies and platforms. Standardizing communication formats (JSON/XML) and adopting RESTful interfaces help bridge these differences.

2.        Data Semantic Inconsistency:

Key terms and identifiers (such as “Citizen ID” or “Asset Code”) may be defined differently across systems. A canonical data model is therefore designed to serve as a reference for core entities like personnel, assets, and hazards.

3.        Security and Governance:

Sensitive data, including citizen information and internal LGU records, must be protected. The API Gateway enforces centralized security policies, such as OAuth 2.0 for authentication and mandatory TLS/SSL for all data in transit.

By addressing these integration aspects, the platform is positioned not only as a stand-alone tool but as a core component of the LGU’s broader information system landscape.

3.1 Agile Scrum Methodology in the Project

The project adopts the Agile Scrum framework because it is designed for complex, evolving systems. Developing a disaster preparedness platform for Barangay San Agustin while integrating a new technology like the Gemini API requires flexibility, rapid feedback, and incremental delivery rather than a rigid, one-time plan.

Work is organized into sprints, typically two weeks long. Each Sprint includes planning, development, testing, and review, enabling the team to validate features such as the simulation engine and AI prompt design on a continuous basis.

Core Scrum Principles Applied

  1. Transparency
  • All work items, priorities, and progress (e.g., items in the Product Backlog and Sprint Backlog) are visible to the Development Team, Product Owner, and Scrum Master.
  1. Inspection
  • Progress is regularly reviewed through daily scrums and sprint reviews to detect deviations early.
  1. Adaptation
  • During sprint retrospectives, the team evaluates what worked well and what needs improvement, then adjusts processes, tools, or priorities accordingly.

Project Roles and Responsibilities

  • Product Owner: Represents LGU 4 and prioritizes features that provide the highest value, such as focusing first on high-risk scenarios like flooding rather than lower-priority hazards.
  • Scrum Master: Facilitates Scrum events, coaches the team on Agile practices, and helps remove impediments, including technical issues (e.g., API key management, deployment blockers) or communication gaps.
  • Development Team: A cross-functional group responsible for architecture design, coding, testing, and integrating the Gemini API. The team self-organizes to decide how to accomplish sprint goals.

C. The Scrum Events (Meetings)

The project utilized the five standard Scrum events to structure work into focused iterations called Sprints (typically two weeks long):

Figure no. 1 Agile Scrum Framework

1.        Sprint Planning: The team selects high-priority items from the Product Backlog and defines a clear Sprint Goal (e.g., “Demonstrate a working prototype of AI-generated flood scenarios”).

2.        Daily Scrum (Stand-up): A short (15-minute) daily meeting where team members share what they did, what they plan to do, and any blockers.

3.        Development Work: Implementation of features, integration tasks, and testing activities needed to deliver the Sprint Goal.

4.        Sprint Review: Presentation of the completed increment to stakeholders, including LGU representatives, to gather feedback and validate alignment with user needs.

5.        Sprint Retrospective: An internal meeting where the team reflects on the previous sprint and identifies concrete actions for improvement.

Scrum Artifacts (Work Products)

  • Product Backlog: A dynamic, prioritized list of all desired features and enhancements for the LGU training system.
  • Sprint Backlog: The set of product backlog items selected for a sprint, along with a plan for delivering them.
  • Increment: The sum of all completed work that meets the Definition of Done, representing a potentially shippable version of the product at the end of each sprint.

The use of Scrum ensures that the project delivers a working, valuable system that is continuously refined based on feedback from actual LGU stakeholders.

3.5 Microservices Architecture

Our diagram shows how the Disaster Preparedness Training & Simulation System is organized as a set of microservices, all accessed through a central API Gateway. The Admin/User interacts only with the API Gateway, which provides a single secure entry point, handles authentication and routing, and forwards each request to the appropriate backend service. All services read and write to a shared Database, which stores scenarios, participant data, module content, results, and resource inventories.

Each colored polygon represents a distinct microservice with a focused responsibility: Training Module Management maintains course content and learning paths; Simulation Event Planning handles scheduling and configuration of exercises; Participants Registration and Attendance manages sign‑ups, demographics, and presence tracking; Scenario‑Based Exercise Design stores and versions realistic disaster scenarios; Evaluation and Scoring System records performance metrics and generates scores; Certification issues and tracks completion certificates; and Resources and Equipment Inventory for Simulation keeps records of available equipment and materials. Because these services are loosely coupled but integrated via the API Gateway and common data store, the system is easier to maintain, scale, and evolve individual services can be updated or extended independently while still working together to deliver a cohesive training and simulation experience.

System (Process): Training & Simulation Microservices System

Represented by the API Gateway, which routes, secures, and aggregates requests to multiple backend services.

Each service focuses on one functional area and communicates with its own dedicated database.

Microservices and Data Stores

Simulation Event Planning Service → Event DB: Manages creation, scheduling, and configuration of training events and stores event details in the Event DB.

Participants Registration Service → Participant DB: Handles participant enrollment, profiles, and attendance, with all related data kept in the Participant DB.

Evaluation & Scoring Service → Evaluation DB: Records assessment results, performance metrics, and feedback in the Evaluation DB.

Certification Service → Certification DB: Issues and tracks certificates, storing completion and credential data in the Certification DB.

Resources & Inventory Service → Inventory DB: Tracks equipment, supplies, and other assets used in simulations, stored in the Inventory DB.

Data Flows (Examples)

From Admin/User to API Gateway: create/update events, register participants, design scenarios, manage modules, record evaluations, issue certifications, update inventory.

From API Gateway to each Service: routed API calls for the specific function (e.g., “Create Event”, “Register Participant”, “Record Score”).

From each Service to its DB: read/write operations for its own domain data, ensuring separation of concerns and scalability.

3.6 DevOps Implementation 

DevOps practices are applied in the development of the Disaster Preparedness Training & Simulation System to tightly integrate coding, testing, and operations around a structured CI/CD pipeline. Developers build the PHP/Laravel web application locally using tools like Cursor/VS Code and XAMPP, then push changes to Git/GitHub, where automated steps handle code build, database migrations, and testing. API tests are executed (e.g., via Postman), and once validated, the pipeline deploys updates through GitHub Actions to hosting environments managed via cPanel moving from local and staging setups to the production server. This automated cycle of code → build → test → deploy → monitor → operate → release minimizes manual errors, speeds up feature delivery, and ensures that the training and simulation system remains stable, quickly updatable, and ready for use in disaster preparedness activities.

Figure no. 6 CI/CD Pipeline

Figure no. 7 Infrastructure as a Code (IaC)

Infrastructure as Code (IaC) is used in the Disaster Preparedness Training & Simulation System to automate how all underlying resources are created and managed, rather than configuring hosting and services manually. The developer commits both application logic and IaC definitions to the Git repository, and the CI/CD pipeline automatically builds, tests, and deploys these changes to the hosting environment. This approach ensures that every component—such as training modules, simulation event planning, participant registration, scenario-based simulations, evaluation and scoring, certification, and resource inventory runs on a consistently configured infrastructure across development, staging, and production. By treating infrastructure as version-controlled code, the system reduces configuration errors, supports scalable growth of training services, and enables rapid, repeatable recovery or environment recreation when issues occur, which is critical for maintaining readiness in disaster preparedness training.

The monitoring and alerting diagram shows how the Disaster Preparedness Training & Simulation System is continuously watched and how problems trigger notifications. Trainers and admins access the system through the web app, and key information from their activities and the backend such as session status, participant activity, and overall system health and latency is collected and sent into the central monitoring component. This monitoring component communicates with the monitoring server, API gateway, and training databases and logs to gather metrics, errors, and performance data in real time. When the monitoring logic detects an issue, such as a failed service, abnormal latency, or a disrupted training session, it passes this condition to the “Issue Detected?” decision step, which then activates the alerting module. The alerting module automatically sends notifications by email and SMS/push messages so that responsible staff can respond quickly and keep the training and simulation environment stable and available.

3.8 Introduction to TOGAF and the Four Architectural Domains 

This section summarizes how TOGAF-inspired practices guide the architecture:

1. Business Architecture

Models existing disaster response protocols and organizational structures, ensuring that the platform supports actual LGU workflows. The Product Owner role, represented by Barangay San Agustin, plays a key part in defining and validating these business needs.

2. Data Architecture

Defines the structure and flow of data used by the system, such as incident histories and personnel information. It also addresses data security, privacy, and consistency across services.

3. Application Architecture

Specifies the software components, their roles, and their interactions. The project uses microservices to Separate concerns and define clear responsibilities for each service.

4. Technology Architecture

Covers cloud infrastructure (e.g., Firebase), network configurations, platforms, and security mechanisms. It includes the use of an API Gateway, encryption protocols (TLS/SSL), and security standards appropriate to LGU data.

These domains collectively ensure that the system is not only technically sound but also organizationally aligned and future-ready.
